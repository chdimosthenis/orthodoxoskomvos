**Apophatic theology**—also known as *negative theology*—is a [theology](/index.php?title=Theology&action=edit&redlink=1 "Theology (page does not exist)") that attempts to describe [God](/God "God") by negation, to speak of God only in absolutely certain terms and to avoid what may not be said. In Orthodox Christianity, apophatic theology is based on the assumption that God's essence is unknowable or ineffable and on the recognition of the inadequacy of human language to describe God. The apophatic tradition in Orthodoxy is often balanced with [cataphatic theology](/Cataphatic_theology "Cataphatic theology")—or *positive theology*—and belief in the [incarnation](/Incarnation "Incarnation"), through which God has revealed himself in the person of [Jesus Christ](/Jesus_Christ "Jesus Christ").

## Apophatic descriptions of God

* From [Scripture](/Holy_Scripture "Holy Scripture")
  + No one has seen or can see God ([John](/Gospel_of_John "Gospel of John") 1:18).
  + He lives in unapproachable light ([1 Tim.](/index.php?title=I_Timothy&action=edit&redlink=1 "I Timothy (page does not exist)") 6:16).
  + His ways are unsearchable and unfathomable ([Job](/index.php?title=Book_of_Job&action=edit&redlink=1 "Book of Job (page does not exist)") 11:7-8; [Romans](/Romans "Romans") 11:33-36).
* By [saints](/Saint "Saint")
  + The true knowledge and vision of God consists in this—in seeing that He is invisible, because what we seek lies beyond all knowledge, being wholly separated by the darkness of incomprehensibility (*The Life of Moses*, [Gregory of Nyssa](/Gregory_of_Nyssa "Gregory of Nyssa")).
  + God is infinite and incomprehensible and all that is comprehensible about Him is His infinity and incomprehensibility (*On the Orthodox Faith*, [John of Damascus](/John_of_Damascus "John of Damascus")).

## History and Development in the Eastern Church

One of the first to articulate the theology in [Christianity](/Introduction_to_Orthodox_Christianity "Introduction to Orthodox Christianity") was the [Apostle Paul](/Apostle_Paul "Apostle Paul"), whose reference to the Unknown God in the book of [Acts](/Acts_of_the_Apostles "Acts of the Apostles") (Acts 17:23) is the foundation of works such as that of [Dionysius the Areopagite](/Dionysius_the_Areopagite "Dionysius the Areopagite"). The [Cappadocian Fathers](/Cappadocian_Fathers "Cappadocian Fathers") of the 4th century, exemplars of this *via negativa*, said that they believed in God, but they did not believe that God exists, at least in the same sense that man exists (notwithstanding the Incarnation). In contrast, making positive statements about the nature of God, which occurs in most other forms of Christian theology, is sometimes called [cataphatic theology](/Cataphatic_theology "Cataphatic theology"). Adherents of the apophatic tradition hold that God is beyond the limits of what humans can understand, and that one should not seek God by means of intellectual understanding, but through a direct experience of the love (in Western Christianity) or the [energies](/index.php?title=Energies_of_God&action=edit&redlink=1 "Energies of God (page does not exist)") (in Eastern Christianity) of God. Apophatic theology can be also seen as an oral tradition. "It must also be recognized that 'forgery' is a modern notion. Like Plotinus and the Cappadocians before him, Dionysius does not claim to be an innovator, but rather a communicator of a tradition." [[1]](http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/pseudo-dionysius-areopagite/)

Apophatic theology played an important role early in the [history of Christianity](/Timeline_of_Church_History "Timeline of Church History"). The [Three Holy Hierarchs](/Three_Holy_Hierarchs "Three Holy Hierarchs") all emphasized the importance of negative theology to an orthodox understanding of God. Later [John of Damascus](/John_of_Damascus "John of Damascus") employed it when he wrote that positive statements about God reveal "not the nature, but the things around the nature." In addition, [Maximus the Confessor](/Maximus_the_Confessor "Maximus the Confessor") maintained that the combination of apophatic theology and [hesychasm](/Hesychasm "Hesychasm")—the practice of keeping stillness—made [theosis](/Theosis "Theosis") or union with God possible. All in all, apophatic statements are crucial to much theology in [Orthodox Christianity](/Orthodox_Christianity "Orthodox Christianity"); the opposite tends to be true in Western Christianity, though there are a few exceptions to this rule.
