Our venerable and God-bearing Father **Maximus the Confessor** (ca. 580-662) was an Orthodox Christian [monk](/Monasticism "Monasticism") and [ascetical](/Asceticism "Asceticism") writer known especially for his courageous fight against the [heresy](/Heresy "Heresy") of [Monothelitism](/Monothelitism "Monothelitism"). His [feast days](/Feast_day "Feast day") in the Church are celebrated on [January 21](/January_21 "January 21") and, for the [translation](/Translation_(relics) "Translation (relics)") of his [relics](/Relics "Relics"), on [August 13](/August_13 "August 13").

## Life

He was born in the region of Constantinople, was well educated, and spent some time in government service before becoming a [monk](/Monk "Monk"), having been a member of the old Byzantine aristocracy and holding the post of Imperial Secretary under Emperor [Heraclius](/Heraclius "Heraclius"). Around 614, he became a monk (later [abbot](/Abbot "Abbot")) at the [monastery](/Monastery "Monastery") of Chrysopolis. During the Persian invasion of the Empire (614), he fled to Africa.

From about 640 on, he became the determined opponent of [Monothelitism](/Monothelitism "Monothelitism"), the [heretical](/Heresy "Heresy") teaching that [Jesus Christ](/Jesus_Christ "Jesus Christ") had only one will. In this, he followed the example of St. [Sophronius of Jerusalem](/Sophronius_I_of_Jerusalem "Sophronius I of Jerusalem"), who was the first to combat this heresy starting in 634.

Maximus supported the Orthodoxy of [Rome](/Church_of_Rome "Church of Rome") on this matter and is said to have exclaimed: "I have the faith of the Latins, but the language of the Greeks." He argued for [Dyothelitism](/Dyothelitism "Dyothelitism"), the Orthodox teaching that Jesus Christ possessed two wills (one divine and one human), rather than the one will posited by Monothelitism.

After [Pyrrhus](/index.php?title=Pyrrhus_of_Constantinople&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pyrrhus of Constantinople (page does not exist)"), the temporarily deposed Monothelite Patriarch of Constantinople, had declared his defeat in a dispute at Carthage (645), Maximus obtained the heresy's condemnation at several local [synods](/Synod "Synod") in Africa, and also worked to have it condemned at the [Lateran Council of 649](/Lateran_Council "Lateran Council"). He was brought to Constantinople in 653, pressured to adhere to the *[Typos](/Typos "Typos")* of Emperor [Constans II](/Constans_II "Constans II"). Refusing to do so, he was exiled to Thrace. (Pope St. [Martin of Rome](/Martin_the_Confessor "Martin the Confessor") was tried around the same time in Constantinople, and thus deposed and exiled to Crimea.)

In 661 Maximus again was brought to the imperial capital and questioned; while there, he had his tongue uprooted and his right hand cut off (to prevent him from preaching or writing the true faith), and then was again exiled to the Caucasus, but died shortly thereafter.

Ultimately, Maximus was exonerated by the [Sixth Ecumenical Council](/Sixth_Ecumenical_Council "Sixth Ecumenical Council") and recognized as a [Father of the Church](/Church_Fathers "Church Fathers").

## Writings

He left many writings (some of which are collected in the [Philokalia](/Philokalia "Philokalia")) that are still widely read today; some are doctrinal, but many more describe the contemplative life and offer spiritual advice. He also wrote widely on liturgical and [exegetical](/Hermeneutics "Hermeneutics") subjects. His theological work was later continued by St. [Simeon the New Theologian](/Simeon_the_New_Theologian "Simeon the New Theologian") and by St. [Gregory Palamas](/Gregory_Palamas "Gregory Palamas").

His writings include:

* *Quaestiones ad Thalassium*—65 questions and answers on difficult passages of [Holy Scripture](/Holy_Scripture "Holy Scripture")
* *Ambigua*—an exegetical work on St. [Gregory the Theologian](/Gregory_the_Theologian "Gregory the Theologian")
* Paraphrases of the works of [Dionysius the Areopagite](/Dionysius_the_Areopagite "Dionysius the Areopagite") (though many of the works that have come down under Maximus' name are now held to be the work of John of Scythopolis, who wrote in the first half of the 6th century, some 100 years before Maximus)
* Several dogmatic treatises against the Monothelites
* *Liber Asceticus*
* *Capita de Caritate*
* *Mystagogia*—a mystical interpretation of the [Divine Liturgy](/Divine_Liturgy "Divine Liturgy")

:   All-wise father Maximus, your teachings have gleamed with light upon all things.

:   Let us the faithful fittingly praise the lover of the [Trinity](/Trinity "Trinity"),

:   And let us cry to him: "Rejoice, herald of the faith."
