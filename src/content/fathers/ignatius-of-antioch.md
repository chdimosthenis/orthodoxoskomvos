Our father among the saints **Ignatius the God-bearer of Antioch** (probably died AD 107) was the third [bishop](/Bishop "Bishop") of Antioch, after the [Apostle Peter](/Apostle_Peter "Apostle Peter") and [Euodios](/Euodios_of_Antioch "Euodios of Antioch"), whom Ignatius succeeded around AD 68. Ignatius, who also called himself **Theophorus** ("[God-bearer](/God-bearer "God-bearer")"), was most likely a disciple of both [Apostles](/Apostle "Apostle") [Peter](/Apostle_Peter "Apostle Peter") and [John](/Apostle_John "Apostle John"). Several of his letters have survived to this day; he is one of the [Apostolic Fathers](/Apostolic_Fathers "Apostolic Fathers") (the earliest group of the [Church Fathers](/Church_Fathers "Church Fathers")), and a [saint](/Saint "Saint") in the [Orthodox Church](/Orthodox_Church "Orthodox Church") (feastday, [December 20](/December_20 "December 20")).

## Martyrdom and Writings

He was arrested by the Roman authorities and transported to Rome to die in the arena. They hoped to make an example of him and thus discourage [Christianity](/Introduction_to_Orthodox_Christianity "Introduction to Orthodox Christianity") from spreading. Instead, he met with and encouraged Christians all along his route, and wrote letters to the Ephesians, Magnesians, Trallians, Philadelphians, Smyrneans, and Romans, as well as a letter to [Polycarp](/Polycarp_of_Smyrna "Polycarp of Smyrna"), who was [bishop](/Bishop "Bishop") of Smyrna and a disciple of [John the Evangelist](/Apostle_John "Apostle John").

These letters proved to be influential in the development of Christian [theology](/index.php?title=Theology&action=edit&redlink=1 "Theology (page does not exist)"), since the number of extant writings from this period of Church history is very small. They bear signs of being written in great haste and without a proper plan, such as run-on sentences and an unsystematic succesion of thought. Ignatius is the first known Christian writer to put great stress on loyality to a single [bishop](/Bishop "Bishop") in each city, who is assisted by both [presbyters](/Presbyter "Presbyter") ([priests](/Priest "Priest")) and [deacons](/Deacon "Deacon"). Earlier writings only mention *either* bishops *or* presbyters, and give the impression that there was usually more than one bishop per [congregation](/Congregation "Congregation"). Ignatius also stresses the value of the [Eucharist](/Eucharist "Eucharist"), calling it "a medicine to immortality." The very strong desire for bloody [martyrdom](/Martyr "Martyr") in the arena, which Ignatius expresses rather graphically in places, may seem quite odd to the modern reader, but an examination of his theology of [soteriology](/Soteriology "Soteriology") shows that he regarded salvation as being from the power and fear of death. So, for him, to try to escape his martyrdom would be to fear death and place himself back under its power.

Nowadays only shorter variants of those seven letters are thought to be authentic writings of Ignatius. Their longer variants are thought to be emendations from the fifth century, created to postumously enlist Ignatius as an unwitting witness into certain theological fights of that age, while the other letters bearing his name and the purported eye-witness account of his martyrdom, are thought to be pure forgeries from around the same time.

## Quotes

"As a pilot calls on winds and a storm-tossed mariner looks homeward, so the times call on you to win your way to God. As God's athlete, be sober; the stake is immortality and eternal life."

## Hymns

[Troparion](/Troparion "Troparion") (Tone 4)

:   By sharing in the ways of the Apostles,
:   you became a successor to their throne.
:   Through the practice of virtue, you found the way to divine contemplation,
:   O inspired one of God;
:   by teaching the word of truth without error, you defended the Faith,
:   even to the shedding of your blood.
:   Hieromartyr Ignatius, entreat Christ God to save our souls.

[Kontakion](/Kontakion "Kontakion") (Tone 3)

:   The stirring celebration of your victorious fight
:   Is an announcement of the One who is to be born of the Virgin.
:   In your eagerness to possess Him forever,
:   You hastened to be devoured by the wild beasts.
:   Therefore, O glorious Ignatius, you were called the bearer of God!
